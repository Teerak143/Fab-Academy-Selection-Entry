Embedded Electronics Architecture

The Embedded Electronics act as the "brain" and "nervous system" of the KineGraph. This part of the project is responsible for taking digital math from the computer and turning it into real electrical power that moves the motors.

The Brain: ESP32 Microcontroller.

The center of the system is the ESP32.. This was chosen because it is much faster than a standard Arduino and has two "cores," which means it can do two things at once. While one core is busy calculating the complicated math for a curve, the other core is making sure the screen and buttons are still working.

The Brain: ESP32 Microcontroller.

Instead of just using a mess of jumper wires, this project uses custom-designed circuit boards.By soldering everything onto a single board, the system becomes much more reliable and won't break if the machine shakes.

Precision Control: Motor Drivers.

To move the pen accurately, the system uses DRV8825 motor drivers. These chips are special because they use 1/16 microstepping.

A normal motor move might be one "big" step.

The driver breaks that one big step into 16 tiny mini-steps.

This is why the KineGraph can draw smooth circles instead of jagged, shaky lines.

How the Signal Flows

The electronics work in a specific order to make sure the drawing is perfect:

The Screen:The user picks a shape using the knob and the screen.

The Math:The ESP32 calculates where the pen needs to go next.

The Pulse:The ESP32 sends a fast electrical "tap" (a pulse) to the motor driver.

The Move:The driver sends a burst of 12V electricity to the NEMA 17 motor, which turns the belt just a tiny fraction of a millimeter.
